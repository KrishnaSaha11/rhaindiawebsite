Refractory formulators value rice husk ash as a high-purity, low-cost source of amorphous silica for insulating castables and linings. To get consistent performance, specify the order carefully. Use this checklist.

## 1. Silica content & purity

Confirm the **SiO₂ percentage** and the level of impurities (alkalis and iron in particular), since these affect refractoriness and the service temperature of the finished lining.

## 2. Crystallinity

For refractory and pozzolanic use alike, **amorphous** silica is preferred. Ask whether the ash is amorphous and how combustion temperature is controlled, as over-burning shifts silica to crystalline forms.

## 3. Particle size & form

Decide whether you need fine ground powder, granules or pellets, and specify the size distribution. Form affects packing density, dust and how the material disperses in a castable.

## 4. Moisture & LOI

Low moisture and low loss-on-ignition keep the material clean and predictable. High LOI (unburned carbon) can interfere with high-temperature service.

## 5. Packing & logistics

Confirm packing (25/50 kg bags or jumbo bags), MOQ, lead time and — for export — full documentation. Consistent packing matters for automated handling.

## 6. Documentation & consistency

Insist on a **NABL-tested data sheet** and ask about batch-to-batch variation. The most common refractory complaint is not a single bad number, but a spec that drifts between deliveries.

> Tip: lock the agreed parameters into the purchase order so every delivery is checked against the same benchmark.

Get these six points right and refractory-grade RHA becomes a reliable, economical part of your formulation.
